## The company

Ulysses builds underwater autonomous drone fleets for ecosystem restoration. Their technology stack sits at the intersection of maritime hardware, autonomy, and what's increasingly being called physical AI. The applications range from coral and kelp restoration to a broader set of ocean ecosystem services that have historically been impossible to perform at scale.

---

## The challenge

Ulysses came to us through Lowercarbon Capital, who had backed the company at seed. They were a high-growth venture-backed startup with a serious technical roadmap and a fundraising arc that was going to require a credible patent portfolio at every stage. They needed a partner who could move at their speed and who could think about IP as something that compounded across rounds, not just something that got filed before the next deck went out.

---

## The approach

We built the portfolio in stages, mapped to Ulysses' roadmap and fundraising milestones. The first filings established priority dates on the core hardware and autonomy stack. International applications followed, designed to preserve global market options as the company scaled.

Strategically, the work emphasized integration claims and system-level inventions. In autonomous maritime tech, the moat usually lives at the integration layer, not in any single subsystem. Filing on the integration meant the resulting patents would be harder to design around, and more valuable in M&A diligence later.

---

## The outcome

Ulysses now has three patent application families with us. Two of those are international (PCT) applications, and both have received positive international search reports.

They closed a $38M Series A from Andreessen Horowitz. The portfolio we built with them was part of the credible foundation that supported the raise.

We're their ongoing patent counsel as the company continues to scale.

## What you can learn from this

- A patent portfolio built in stages, mapped to fundraising milestones, holds up under venture diligence in a way that a one-time filing rush does not.
- For technologies with global commercial markets, international filings should be planned from the start, not bolted on later.
- The moat in autonomous and integrated systems usually lives at the integration layer. File there.
- Long-term patent counsel relationships compound. Each round of fundraising builds on the last, and the cost of getting it right early is much lower than the cost of fixing it later.
